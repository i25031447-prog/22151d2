Taste the Soul of Cantonese Cuisine

Signature Dishes

Dim Sum

Har Gow (crystal shrimp dumplings) and Siu Mai (pork and shrimp dumplings) are the iconic duo. Traditionally enjoyed with morning tea, dim sum is a social ritual and an art form in Guangzhou.

Roast Goose

Guangzhou's roast goose is legendary — crispy, golden skin enveloping tender, juicy meat marinated with five-spice and other aromatics. The famous Panyu roast goose is considered the finest in all of China.

Wonton Noodles

Springy egg noodles topped with plump shrimp and pork wontons in a clear, savory broth. The dish is all about balance — the texture of the noodles, the freshness of the filling, and the depth of the soup.

Slow-Cooked Soup

Slow-cooked soups simmered for hours with carefully selected herbs, meats, and vegetables. These nourishing tonics are believed to balance the body and promote health — a philosophy deeply rooted in traditional Chinese medicine.

Char Siu (BBQ Pork)

Glossy, caramelized Char Siu is a Cantonese masterpiece. The pork is marinated in honey, soy sauce, and five-spice, then roasted until the edges caramelize perfectly. Enjoy it on its own, with rice, or inside Char Siu Bao (steamed BBQ pork buns). Every Cantonese roast shop in Guangzhou has its own closely guarded recipe passed down through generations.

Rice Rolls (Chang Fen)

Thin, silky steamed rice noodles rolled around fillings like shrimp, pork, or beef, drizzled with sweet soy sauce. A beloved breakfast staple across Guangzhou, Chang Fen showcases the Cantonese mastery of delicate steaming techniques. The best versions are found in small neighborhood shops where vendors steam them to order right before your eyes.

Essential Cooking Techniques

Steaming

The soul of dim sum. Gentle steaming preserves the natural flavors and nutrients of ingredients, producing delicate textures that define Cantonese cuisine.

Stir-Frying (Wok Hei)

High-heat wok cooking creates the prized "wok hei" — a smoky char that gives Cantonese stir-fries their distinctive aroma and flavor.

Double-Boiling

Slow soups are double-boiled in ceramic pots for 3-6 hours, extracting maximum flavor while keeping the broth crystal clear and nutrient-rich.

Roasting (Siu)

Cantonese roasting produces glossy, caramelized meats. Specialized ovens and marinades create the signature flavors of Char Siu, roast duck, and roast goose.
