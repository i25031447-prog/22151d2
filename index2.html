About Guangzhou

Guangzhou, the capital of Guangdong Province, is one of China's most vibrant metropolises with a history spanning over 2,200 years. Known as the "City of Rams," it has served as a major port on the Maritime Silk Road and remains a crucial hub for international trade. Today, Guangzhou seamlessly blends ancient traditions with cutting-edge modernity, offering visitors an unforgettable experience of Cantonese culture, world-class cuisine, and stunning architecture.

As the birthplace of Cantonese cuisine, Guangzhou is recognized as a UNESCO City of Gastronomy. The city's culinary scene ranges from humble street-side noodle stalls to Michelin-starred restaurants, all united by an obsession with fresh ingredients and delicate flavors. The iconic Yum Cha (morning tea) tradition, where families gather over bamboo steamers of dim sum, is a daily ritual that embodies the Cantonese philosophy of savoring life's pleasures.

Beyond food, Guangzhou captivates visitors with its incredible diversity of experiences.

Must-See Attractions

Canton Tower

The tallest TV tower in China at 600m, offering observation decks, a thrilling sky walk, and dazzling LED night shows over the Pearl River.

Sun Yat-sen Memorial Hall

A magnificent octagonal building dedicated to the founding father of modern China, surrounded by lush gardens in the heart of the city.

Chimelong Paradise

One of China's largest theme parks featuring world-class roller coasters, water rides, and live entertainment for all ages.

Did You Know?

In 2014, Guangzhou became the second city in China to be designated as a UNESCO Creative City of Gastronomy. The city is widely regarded as the birthplace of Cantonese cuisine, one of the Eight Great Cuisines of China. With over 60,000 restaurants, it boasts the highest restaurant density of any city in the country.

Standing at 600 meters tall, the Canton Tower is nearly twice the height of the Eiffel Tower (330m). It was the tallest TV tower in the world upon completion in 2010 and remains the tallest structure in Guangzhou. Its unique twisting design has earned it the nickname "Supermodel" among locals.

The art of dim sum (Dianxin in Mandarin) originated in Guangzhou's teahouses along the ancient Silk Road. Traditional dim sum chefs train for years to master the delicate folding techniques. From the translucent Har Gow to the open-topped Siu Mai, each piece is a miniature work of culinary art.

Founded in 214 BC, Guangzhou is one of the oldest ports in the world. As the starting point of the Maritime Silk Road, it has welcomed merchants from Rome, Arabia, India, and Southeast Asia for over two millennia. Today, the Canton Fair (since 1957) continues this tradition as China's largest trade exhibition.

Guangzhou has had many names throughout history including Panyu, Xingguang, and most famously "Canton" — the Western name derived from Portuguese pronunciation. Locals proudly call it "Yangcheng" (City of Rams) after a legend where five immortal rams brought rice to save the city from famine.
